# Hooks

A hook is the first sentence or two that gives the reader a reason to keep reading.

This file is local taste guidance for `write-post`. Use the `hook-writing` skill for the general hook workflow, strategy set, and critique rubric.

## Bar

- short
- simple
- concrete
- inviting
- tension-bearing

The hook should open tension, not perform cleverness.

Use `taste-memory.md` as current taste guidance. Prefer a hook with a turn over a descriptive opening that only creates atmosphere.

## Useful hook types

- **Lived friction**: starts from something the writer experienced.
  - `I kept coming back and reconstructing the work.`
- **Reader pain**: names the reader's problem directly.
  - `You do not have a writing problem. You have a restart problem.`
- **Misconception reversal**: corrects the obvious diagnosis.
  - `The blank page was not the hard part.`
- **But turn**: uses a reversal to make the premise feel discovered, not announced.
  - `I tried to make the agent smarter, but the answer was already sitting in Contacts.app.`
- **Scene**: drops the reader into a concrete moment.
  - `The draft was open. I still had no idea where I had left off.`
- **Mechanism**: names the system idea in plain language.
  - `A good writing workflow leaves breadcrumbs.`
- **Question**: asks only when the question is sharper than the answer.
  - `What if the real draft is the state you leave behind?`

## Hook lab output

Return 3-5 candidates. For each:

- hook sentence
- type
- reader tension
- why it works or why it might fail

## Gate

Pick one working hook before drafting. Reject hooks that:

- explain too much
- sound like a headline
- start with generic context
- use cleverness without pain
- set atmosphere without reaching the argument
- could open any article on the internet

Also reject a hook if the second sentence cannot pay it off. A hook should open a loop the piece can honestly close, not create a flashy detour.

## Taste Checks

- Check whether a `but` turn would make the tension more human.
- Preserve exact articles when they sharpen the claim: `A happy path is not an automation` is stronger than a more generic version.
- Do not let lyrical facts delay the post's real problem.
