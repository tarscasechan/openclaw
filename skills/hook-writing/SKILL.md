# Hook Writing

## Core rule

Open where the reader starts caring. A hook should create tension, curiosity, surprise, stakes, or motion without tricking the reader.

Do not write a generic introduction. Do not warm up. Start close to the pressure point.

## Hook strategies

Use the strategy that fits the piece, not the flashiest one.

- **Question hook:** Ask a question the reader actually wants answered. Avoid obvious or fake questions.
- **Shocking statistic or fact:** Use a number or fact that changes the reader’s frame. Verify it if the piece depends on factual accuracy.
- **Quotation hook:** Borrow authority only when the quote is strong, specific, and not overused. Prefer original phrasing when possible.
- **Anecdote or narrative hook:** Drop the reader into a concrete moment: person, place, action, consequence.
- **Bold or provocative statement:** Challenge a belief directly, then earn the claim quickly.
- **Common misconception hook:** Name what people believe, then reveal the useful correction.
- **Specific pain hook:** Start with the annoying reality the reader already recognizes.
- **Contrarian hook:** Say the thing that sounds wrong until the piece proves it right.
- **Tiny scene hook:** Use one vivid detail that implies the larger problem.
- **Outcome hook:** Lead with what changes for the reader if they keep reading.

## Workflow

1. Identify the reader, topic, format, and desired tone.
2. Find the real tension: surprise, pain, contradiction, risk, desire, or unanswered question.
3. Draft 5-10 materially different hooks before choosing one.
4. Prefer specific hooks over broad claims.
5. Cut throat-clearing phrases like “In today’s world,” “Since the beginning of time,” “This essay will,” and “It is important to note.”
6. Make the first sentence carry force by itself.
7. Make the next sentence pay off the hook; do not let the opening become clickbait.
8. If the hook uses a fact, statistic, quote, or named source, verify it or mark it as unverified.
9. Choose the hook that best fits the reader tension and promise of the piece, not the one that sounds cleverest.
10. Tighten until the opening sounds natural when read aloud.

## Selection gate

A hook passes only if:

- the intended reader is clear
- the tension is specific
- the promise matches what the piece will actually deliver
- the second sentence can pay off the first without a bait-and-switch
- any factual claim is verified or explicitly marked unverified
- the hook sounds like the author, not a template

## What good hooks do

- create a question in the reader’s mind
- expose a contradiction or misconception
- make an abstract topic concrete
- show stakes quickly
- promise useful movement
- match the piece’s actual argument
- sound like the author, not a marketing template

## What to avoid

- fake urgency
- bait-and-switch curiosity gaps
- broad dictionary-style openings
- stale quotes
- unsupported statistics
- rhetorical questions with obvious answers
- “everything you know is wrong” unless the piece truly proves it
- cleverness that delays the point
- hooks that oversell a modest piece

## Output patterns

When asked to generate hooks, return grouped options:

- **Direct:** clear, useful, low-drama
- **Contrarian:** challenges a default belief
- **Narrative:** starts with a scene or moment
- **Pain-first:** names the reader’s frustration
- **Curiosity:** opens a loop the piece can close

When asked to critique a hook, judge it against:

1. Does it make the reader care?
2. Is it specific?
3. Is it honest about what the piece delivers?
4. Does the next sentence have somewhere to go?
5. Can it be said more simply?

## Output standard

Return hooks that are sharp, honest, and usable. Prefer one excellent plain hook over five flashy weak ones.
If context is missing, make reasonable assumptions and label them briefly.

## Mini eval cases

Use these as quick self-checks when changing this skill or judging hook output:

- Generic intro: replace “In today’s fast-paced world...” with a concrete reader tension.
- Fake rhetorical question: cut or rewrite a question whose answer is obvious.
- Unsupported statistic: verify it before use or mark it unverified.
- Exact working hook: preserve wording when a small article or turn sharpens the claim.
- Oversold hook: tone it down when the piece cannot prove the promised reversal.
