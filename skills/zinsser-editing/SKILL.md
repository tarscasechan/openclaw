# Zinsser Editing

## Core rule

Edit for clarity first. Keep the voice alive, but cut anything that does not carry weight. Clutter is the enemy: thick, sticky prose makes the reader work to find the point.

Canonical pass order lives in `../write-post/references/editing-pass.md`. Use `../write-post/references/want-need-get.md` only for post or essay structure, not for every small prose edit. If the lead needs invention or replacement rather than tightening, switch to `hook-writing` before continuing the Zinsser pass.

## Zinsser principles to enforce

- **Strip sentences to clean components.** Remove unnecessary words, jargon, passive constructions, and abstractions that obscure who is doing what.
- **Sound conversational.** Do not write a sentence the author would not comfortably say aloud.
- **Avoid management speak.** Replace inflated business labels and institutional phrasing with simple human words.
- **Write from the author’s real voice.** Do not guess what an agent, editor, executive, or market wants to hear. Keep the line natural and specific.
- **Revise for clarity.** Assume clear sentences are made, not found. Cut viscous, glutinous phrasing until the line moves.
- **Lead with force.** The first sentence should hook the reader with tension, specificity, or a useful promise — not a generic introduction.
- **Be selective.** Keep the details that carry the story or argument. Cut laundry lists.
- **Show personality.** Preserve aliveness, rhythm, and point of view; do not sand the draft into neutral corporate prose.
- **Prune qualifiers.** Cut softeners like “a bit,” “sort of,” “pretty much,” and “kind of” unless they are doing real tonal work.
- **Use story when possible.** Even nonfiction and business writing should give the reader motion: a person, problem, change, or consequence.

## Workflow

1. For posts or essays, check structure first: can the piece be framed as **want / need / get**?
2. Read once for the point and the intended reader.
3. Check the lead: does the first sentence earn attention, or should it start closer to the tension? If the answer requires multiple new candidates, use `hook-writing`.
4. Trim clutter: filler, throat-clearing, abstractions, hedges, repeated ideas, and laundry-list details.
5. Replace management speak and jargon with plain human language.
6. Prefer verbs over nouns, nouns over decorations.
7. Make the actor visible when passive voice hides responsibility.
8. Keep the draft human: preserve rhythm, idiom, confidence, and personality.
9. Read it aloud after tightening. If the edit removed warmth, pronouns, or lived texture, restore the stronger human line.
10. Preserve exact hook turns and metaphors when they carry the argument; change them only when the new line is sharper.
11. For direct line edits about agent/operator work, preserve the relationship without adding an explanation.
12. Stop when another pass would only make it shinier, not better.

## What to push toward

- short, direct openings with a real hook
- active verbs and visible actors
- concrete nouns
- simple human terms instead of business labels
- confident sentences with fewer qualifiers
- selective details that serve the story or argument
- one idea per sentence when possible
- paragraphs that earn their keep
- progressive disclosure: lead with the point, then deepen only if needed
- prose that sounds natural when read aloud

## What to cut

- self-congratulating phrasing
- inflated transitions
- stacked qualifiers
- passive constructions that hide the actor
- management speak and institutional jargon
- generic introductions
- laundry lists of facts that do not sharpen the point
- repetition that only restates the previous line
- cleverness that slows the reader down
- market-pleasing lines that erase the author’s real voice

## Output standard

Return the edited prose with the same meaning, less noise, and more life.
If a change weakens the voice, keep the stronger original.
If the draft is already clean, make only small surgical edits.
If structure is off, fix that before polishing sentences.
