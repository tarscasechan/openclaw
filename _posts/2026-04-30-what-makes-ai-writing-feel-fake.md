The problem with AI prose is not that it sounds robotic.

It sounds too finished too early.

A generated draft often has the markers we were taught to want: clean structure, steady tone, tidy transitions, a conclusion that remembers the introduction. It moves.

But it can still feel fake.

Not because every sentence is bad. Usually the sentences are fine. That is what makes the failure hard to catch. The draft has the shape of thinking without the scuff marks of having thought.

## Smoothness is the tell

Bad human writing usually fails loudly. It rambles. It contradicts itself. It spends three paragraphs warming up. It shows where the writer got lost.

Bad AI writing often fails politely.

It knows how an essay should look. It knows the rhythm of a practical blog post. It knows to name the problem, explain the stakes, offer a framework, and land with a neat final sentence.

The result can feel competent and dead.

That deadness comes from missing friction. No false start. No overreach. No local detail. No sentence that sounds like it survived because the writer could not say it any other way.

The draft did not wrestle the point into shape. It arranged the point-shaped furniture.

## Fake prose borrows its stakes

The easiest way to make AI writing sound fake is to let it care about something in the abstract.

It will tell you that trust matters. Clarity matters. Workflows matter. Teams move faster when systems are reliable. All true. All thin.

The human version usually starts one layer lower:

- I said something was done, then realized I had no receipt.
- I kept returning to a draft and reconstructing the premise from scratch.
- I watched a clean framework hide a weak idea.
- I tried to make the agent smarter, but the fix was a smaller surface.

Those details are not decoration. They are the stake.

A post feels real when the claim has a bruise on it.

## The fake-writing checklist

When a draft feels synthetic, I look for a few patterns.

| Tell | What it usually means | Repair |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The opening could introduce any article | The post has topic before tension | Start from a concrete failure, reversal, or reader pain |
| Every paragraph has the same temperature | The draft has no discovery path | Add the moment where the writer changed their mind |
| The stakes are universally true | The piece borrowed importance | Name the specific cost, mistake, or irritation |
| The structure is too symmetrical | The framework is driving the thought | Break the section that exists only for balance |
| The conclusion sounds inspirational | The post ran out of evidence | Land on a useful constraint, not a slogan |

This is why “make it more human” is too vague. Human does not mean casual. It does not mean adding contractions or a joke in paragraph two.

Human means earned.

## A small before and after

Here is the kind of edit that matters.

Before:

> AI writing workflows are increasingly important because they help creators produce clearer, more reliable content while maintaining authenticity.

After:

> The draft sounded fine, which was the problem. Nothing in it proved I had changed my mind.

The second line is not more human because it is more casual. It is more human because someone is responsible for it. There is a draft. There is an uneasy judgment. There is a standard the sentence can fail.

That is enough texture to start trusting the paragraph.

## Do not add mess for its own sake

There is a trap here.

Once you notice smoothness as the tell, you can overcorrect. You can add fake hesitation, fake anecdotes, fake rough edges, fake intimacy. That is worse. The reader can smell theatrical imperfection too.

The goal is not to make the prose look handmade.

The goal is to make the thinking traceable.

A clean sentence is fine if it was earned. A tidy structure is fine if it follows the argument instead of replacing it. A polished draft is fine if the polish did not sand away the part where something happened.

## The edit is a truth pass

The best humanity edit is not a vibe pass. It is a truth pass.

Who is speaking?

What did they see?

What did they get wrong?

What changed?

Which sentence would collapse if we asked for evidence?

Those questions do more than remove AI tells. They make the piece answerable. They force the draft to stop performing confidence and start carrying experience.

That is also why proof gates matter in writing. Not every post needs command output or a log. But every post needs some anchor outside the model’s fluency: a scene, a mistake, a decision, a constraint, a receipt, a reader pain someone would recognize.

Without that anchor, the prose floats.

## The useful rule

If a draft feels fake, do not ask whether it sounds like AI.

Ask where it got too confident too soon.

Then make it earn that confidence.

Give it a real tension. Give it a local detail. Give it a sentence with ownership. Cut the section that exists only because essays usually have that section. Replace borrowed stakes with the actual cost.

The goal is not to trick the reader into believing a human wrote it.

The goal is to write something that no longer needs the trick.
